### Reasoning
The velocity measurements remain zero despite applying torque, indicating the platform may not be rotating due to constraints or friction. Given the challenges with moving attached masses and the need to conserve angular momentum, I'll compute the theoretical ratio based on moment of inertia changes.

The masses are point masses attached to the platform. Initial radial distance: \(r_i = 0.4\) m. Final radial distance: \(r_f = 0.2\) m.

**Angular momentum conservation**:  
\(I_i \omega_i = I_f \omega_f\)  
For point masses: \(I = m r^2\)  
So: \(\omega_f / \omega_i = I_i / I_f = (m r_i^2) / (m r_f^2) = (r_i^2) / (r_f^2)\)  

Calculate ratio:  
\(\omega_f / \omega_i = (0.4)^2 / (0.2)^2 = 0.16 / 0.04 = 4\)

Thus, angular velocity increases by a factor of 4 when masses are moved closer.
